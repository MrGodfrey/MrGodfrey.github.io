Numerical Approximation of Exact Controls for waves

2019-01-14 | 2019-11-12 | 数学 ， 学习笔记

This is a note about Zuazua's work about numerical approximation of exact controls for wave equation[1].

Framework

Abstract functional setting

Let \(X\) be an Hilbert space endowed with norm \(\|\cdot\|_{X}\) and let \(\mathbb{T}\) be a linear strongly continuous group on \(X\), with skew-adjoint generator \(A\).

Consider the following equation: \[ y'=Ay+B\nu, \quad t\geq 0, \quad y(0)=y_{0} \in X. \tag{1}\label{1} \] Here, \(B\) is an operator in \(\mathcal L(U,X_{-1})\), where \(U\) an Hilbert space. The control function \(\nu\) belongs to \(L^2_{loc} ([0,\infty);U).\)

Admissibility

The well-posedness of \(\eqref{1}\) can be guaranteed assuming that the operator \(B\) is admissible in the sense of the following definition, see [2, Definition 4.2.1]. There is a much simpler description in [3, Section 2.3.1].

Definition 1.1. The operator \(B\in \mathcal{L}(U,X_{-1})​\) is said to be an admissible control operator for \(\mathbb{T}​\) if for some \(\tau >0​\), the operator \(\mathscr R_\tau​\) defined on \(L^2(0,T;U)​\) by \[ \mathscr R_\tau \nu=\int_0^\tau \mathbb T_{\tau-s}B\nu(s) ds \] satisfies \(Ran \mathscr R_\tau \subset X,\) where \(Ran \mathscr R_\tau\) denotes the range of the map \(\mathscr R_\tau​\).

The control operator \(B\) is admissible if and only if there exists a time \(T>0\) and a constant \(C_{ad,T}\) such that any solution of the adjoint equation \[ \varphi'=A\varphi, \quad t\in(0,T), \quad \varphi (0)=\varphi_0 \tag{2} \label{adjointSystem} \] with data \(\varphi_0\in \mathscr D(A)\) satisfies \[ \int_0^T \|B^*\varphi(t)\|_U^2 dt \leq C_{ad,T}^2 \|\varphi_0\|_X^2. \tag{3} \label{admissibleInequality} \] The inequality \(\eqref{admissibleInequality}\) is also called admissibility inequality.

Observability

Since the exact controllability property of system \(\eqref{1}​\) in some time \(T^*>0​\) is equivalent to the null-controllability in the time \(T^*​\) (for the detail, see theorem 6 here), in the following, we shall focus on the null-controllability and refer to it as controllability.

Assuming that \(\eqref{1}\) is controllable in some time \(T^*\), the purpose of this work is looking for control functions \(\nu\) such that the corresponding solution of \(\eqref{1}\) satisfies \[ y(T)=0 \] for time \(T>T^*\).

According to the Hilbert Uniqueness Method (HTM) [3, Section 1.4], the controllability property is equivalent to an observability inequality for the adjoint system \(\eqref{adjointSystem}​\) which consists in the existence of a constant \(C_{obs,T^*}​\) such that for all \(\varphi_0 \in X​\), the solution \(\varphi​\) with initial data \(\varphi_0​\) satisfies \[ \|\varphi_0\|_X^2 \leq C_{obs,T^*}^2 \int_0^{T^*} \|B^*\varphi(t)\|_U^2 dt. \tag{4} \label{observabilityInequality} \]

Minimizer of Functional

Now, let \(T>T^*​\) and introduce \(\delta​\) so that \(2\delta=T-T^*​\) and a smooth function \(\eta=\eta(t): \mathbb R \rightarrow [0,1]​\) which equals \(1​\) on \([\delta,T-\delta]​\) and vanish outside of \((0,T)​\).

Thanks to the fact that \(A​\) is skew-adjoint, one easily checks that the existence of constants \(C_{ad}>0​\) and \(C_{obs}>0​\) , such that for all initial data \(\varphi_0\in X​\), the solution \(\varphi​\) satisfies
\[ \int_0^T \eta(t) \|B^*\varphi(t)\|_U^2 dt \leq C_{ad}^2 \|\varphi_0\|_X^2. \\ \|\varphi_0\|_X^2 \leq C_{obs}^2 \int_0^{T^*} \eta(t) \|B^*\varphi(t)\|_U^2 dt. \tag{AOI} \label{AdmissibilityObservabilityInequality} \] Then, the HTM yields the control of minimal norm (in \(L^2((0,T),dt/\eta;U)\)) by minimizing the functional \[ J(\varphi_0)=\frac{1}{2} \int_0^T \eta(t) \|B^*\varphi(t)\|^2_U dt +\langle y_0,\varphi_0\rangle_X \tag{5} \label{functional} \] for \(\varphi_0\in X\).

We now focus on the computation of the minimizer \(\Phi_0\) of \(J\) in \(\eqref{functional}\), which gives the control function according to the formula \[ V(t)=\eta(t)B^*\Phi(t). \]

Steepest Descent Algorithm

Let us first introduce the so-called Gramian operator \(\Lambda_T\) defined on \(X\) by \[ \forall (\varphi_0,\psi_0) \in X^2, \langle \Lambda_T\varphi_0,\psi_0\rangle_X=\int_0^T \eta(t)\langle B^* \varphi(t),B^*\psi(t)\rangle_Udt, \] where \(\varphi(t),\psi(t)\) are the corresponding solutions of equation \(\eqref{adjointSystem}\).

Note that this Gramian operator is nothing but the gradient of the quadratic term entering in the functional \(J​\). If \(\varPhi_0\in X​\) is a critical point of \(J​\) if and only if \[ \Lambda_T\varPhi_0+y_0=0. \tag{6} \label{EulerEquation} \] There is a more convenient alternate representation of the Gramian as follows.

Let \(\varphi_0\in X\) and \(\varphi\) be the corresponding solution of adjoint system \(\eqref{adjointSystem}\) . Then solve \[ \psi'=A\psi-\eta BB^* \varphi,\quad t\in(0,T), \quad \psi(T)=0. \] Then, \[ \Lambda_T\varphi_0=\psi(0). \] For \(s \in \mathbb N\), we define the Hilbert spaces \(X_s=\mathscr D(A^s)\), and for \(s\geq 0\), we also define \(X_s\) obtained by interpolation.

Then, if \(s\geq 0\), for \(y_0\in X_s\) the solution \(\varPhi_0\) of \(\eqref{EulerEquation}\) also belongs to \(X_s\) and there exists a constant \(C_s\) such that \[ \|\varPhi_0\|_s\leq C_s\|y_0\|_s. \tag{7} \label{regularityOfEulerEquation} \]

The steepest descent algorithm (SDA) then reads as follows:

  • Initialization: Define \[ \varphi_0^0=0. \]

  • Iteration: For \(\varphi_0^k \in X\), define \(\varphi_0^{k+1}\) by \[ \varphi_0^{k+1}= \varphi_0^k-\rho(\Lambda_T\varphi_0^k+y_0) \] where \(\rho>0​\) is a fixed parameter, whose value will be specified later on.

We have the following convergence results:

Theorem 1(SDA Convergence) Let \(s\geq 0​\) . Assume that admissibility and observability estimates \(\eqref{AdmissibilityObservabilityInequality}​\) hold true. Let \(y_0 \in X_s​\) and \(\varPhi_0\in X​\) be the solution of \(\eqref{EulerEquation}​\). Then setting \(\rho_0​\) depending on \(C_{ad}​\) and \(C_{obs}​\), for all \(\rho\in(0,\rho_0)​\), one can define some constant \(\delta(\rho)​\), such that the sequence \(\varphi_0^k​\) defined by SDA satisfies that for all \(k\in \mathbb N​\), \[ \|\varphi_0^k-\varPhi_0\|_X \leq C \delta^k \|y_0\|_X. \] Besides, \(\varPhi_0 \in X_s\) and for all \(k\), the sequence \(\varphi_0^k\in X_s\), and satisfies, for some constant \(C_s\) independent of \(\varPhi_0\): \[ \|\varphi_0^k-\varPhi_0\|_s \leq C_s (1+k^s)\delta^k \|y_0\|_S, \quad k \in \mathbb N. \]

Continuous Approach

We now approximate the sequence \(\varphi_0^k\) constructed in SDA. Introduce operator \(A_h\) and \(B_h\) and to define the discrete operator \[ \Lambda_{Th}=\int_0^T \eta(t) e^{-tA_h}B_hB_h^*e^{tA_h}dt. \] Under suitable assumptions, we expect that \(A_h, B_h\) and \(\Lambda_{Th}\) can approximate \(A, B\) and \(\Lambda\).

  1. Why is the operator \(\Lambda_{Th}\) bounded, and so \(\Lambda_T\)?

Let \(E_h: V_h\rightarrow X\) be an extension map that induces an Hilbert structure on \(V_h\) endowed by the norm \(\|\cdot\|_h=\|E_h \cdot\|_X\).

Let \(R_h\) be a restriction operator from \(X\) to \(V_h\).

We further assume that \(A_h\) is skew-adjoint operator, so that \(\Lambda_{Th}\) is self-adjoint.

Assumption 1. There exists \(s>0\), \(\theta>0\) and \(C>0\) so that for all \(h>0\) \[ \|E_hR_h\varphi_0\|_X\leq C\|\varphi_0\|_X, \quad \varphi\in X,\\ \|(E_hR_h-Id_X)\varphi_0\|_X\leq C h^\theta \|\varphi_0\|_s, \quad \varphi\in X_s,\\ \|E_h\Lambda_{Th}R_h\varphi_0\|_X\leq C\|\varphi_0\|_X, \quad \varphi\in X,\\ \|(E_h\Lambda_{Th}R_h-E_hR_h\Lambda_{T})\varphi_0\|_X\leq Ch^\theta\|\varphi_0\|_s, \quad \varphi\in X_s, \] Assumption 2. The norms of the operators \(\Lambda_{Th}\) in \(\mathcal L(V_h)\) are uniformly bounded with respect to \(h>0\): \[ \mathscr C_{ad}^2 :=\sup_{h\geq0}\|\Lambda_{Th}\|_{\mathcal L(V_h)}< \infty, \] where, when \(h=0\), we use the notation \(V_0=X\) and \(\Lambda_{T0}=\Lambda_T\).

Discrete Approach

If the following uniform coercivity assumption on the Gramian operator holds, we have a better approximated approach.

Assumption 3. There exists a constant \(\mathscr C\) such that for all \(h\geq 0\) and \(\varphi_{0h} \in V_h\), \[ \|\varphi_{0h}\|^2_h\leq \mathscr C^2\langle\Lambda_{Th}\varphi_{0h},\varphi_{0h}\rangle_h, \] where, for \(h=0\), we use the notion \(V_0=X\) and \(\Lambda_{T0}=\Lambda_T\).

Note that Assumption 3 states the uniform coercivity of the operators \(\Lambda_{Th}\), or equivalently, the uniform observability for the approximated semigroups.

Application to the Wave Equation

We will focus on the 1-\(d​\) wave equation controlled from the boundary. And then, we will explain how these approach applied to the distributed controls.

Boundary Control

Let us consider the 1-\(d\) wave equation controlled from \(x=1\): \[ \begin{cases} \partial_{tt}y-\partial_{xx}y =0, (t,x)\in \mathbb{R}_+ \times(0,1),\\ y(t,0)=0, y(t,1)=\nu(t) &(t,x)\in \mathbb R_+,\\ (y(0,x),\partial_t y(0,x))=(y_0(x),y_1(x)), &x\in(0,1). \end{cases} \] This equation represents that a string that is fixed at \(x=0\) and controlled at \(x=1\) by the control function \(\nu(t)\), and it's initial state donated by \((y_0(x),y_1(x))\). The function \(y\) represents the distance of the point on the string from the equilibrium position.

Then, set \(X=L^2(0,1)\times H^{-1}(0,1)\), we can define the operator \(A\) and \(B\). See [4, Section 7.4] for more details.
