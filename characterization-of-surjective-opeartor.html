Characterization of surjective opeartor

2018-11-18 | 数学 ， 学习笔记

This article provides a useful characterization of surjective operator, which is called the method of a priori estimates.

The main result concerning surjective operators is the following. For the proofs we refer the reader to [1, Theorem 2.20, page 47].

Theorem 1 Let \(E\) and \(F\) be two Banach spaces. Let \(A: D(A) \subset E \rightarrow F\) be an unbounded linear operator that is densely defined and closed. The following properties are equivalent:

  1. \(A\) is surjective,
  2. there is a constant \(C\) such that \(\|v\| \leq C \|A^\star v\|\), for all \(v\) in \(D(A^\star)\),
  3. \(N(A^\star)={0}\) and \(R(A^\star)\) is closed.

If restricting our attention to Hilbert spaces, there is a further conclusion, which was proved by Hormander [2, Lemma 4.1.1, page 78].

Theorem 2 Let \(E\) and \(F\) be two Hilbert spaces. Let \(A: D(A) \subset E \rightarrow F\) be an unbounded linear operator that is densely defined and closed. Let \(K\) be a closed subspace of \(F\) containing the range \(R(A)\) of \(A\). Then \(K=R(A)\) if and only if for some constant \(C\) \[ \|f\|_F \leq C \|A^\star f\|_E, \quad f\in K\cap D(A^\star). \tag{1} \label{1} \] The following lemma gives a sufficient condition for \(A^{\star\star}=A\), which can be found in [1, Theorem 3.24, page 72].

Lemma 3 Let \(E\) and \(F\) be two reflexive Banach spaces. Let \(A: D(A) \subset E \rightarrow F\) be an unbounded linear operator that is densely defined and closed. Then \(D(A^\star)\) is dense in \(F^\star\). Thus \(A^{\star \star}\) is well defined and it may also be viewed as an unbounded operator from \(E\) into \(F\). Then we have \(A^{\star\star}=A\).

Proof of Theorem 2 First assume that \(\eqref{1}\) is valid and let \(g\in K\). Since \(A^{\star\star}=A\), the equation \(Au=g\) is equivalent to the identity \[ (u,A^\star f)_E=(g,f)_F, \quad f \in D(A^\star) \] It suffices to prove \[ |(g,f)_F|\leq C\|g\|_F \|A^\star f\|_E, \quad f\in D(A^\star). \tag{2} \label{2} \] Indeed, let \(L\) be the antilinear functional \(A^\star f\rightarrow(g,f)_F\) from \(R(A^*)\). The map \(L\) is well defined, since \(A^\star\) is injective by \(\eqref{1}\). An application of the Hahn-Banach theorem to \(L\) shows that there exists a linear functional \(\tilde{L}\) from \(E\), which is bounded by \(\eqref{2}\). According to Riesz representation theorem, there exists \(u \in E\) such that \((u,A^\star f)_E=(g,f)_F\) for all \(f \in D(A^\star)\). Thus, the equation \(Au=g\) has a solution \(u\) with \[ \|u\|_E \leq C \|g\|_F. \]

To prove \(\eqref{2}\) we first note that, if \(f\) is orthogonal to \(K\), we have \((g,f)_F=0\), and \((A^\star f, v)=(f,Av)=0\) for all \(v\in D(A)\), since \(R(A)\subset K\), which implies \(A^{\star}f=0\). Hence it is sufficient to prove \(\eqref{2}\) when \(f \in K \cap D(A^\star)\) and then it follows immediately from \(\eqref{1}\).

Conversely, assuming that \(R(A)=K\), we must prove that the set \[ B=\{f;f\in K \cap D(A^\star), \|A^\star f\|_E \leq 1\} \] is bounded. To do so it is sufficient to prove that \(B\) is weakly bounded in \(K\), this means that \(|(f,g)|_F\) is bounded when \(f \in B\) for every fixed \(g \in K\) (see following lemma). But by hypothesis we can choose \(u \in D(A)\) such that \(Au=g\), and this implies \[ |(f,g)_F|=|(A^\star f,u)|_E \leq \|u\|_E, \quad f\in B \] The theorem is proved. \(\Box\)

Lemma 4 (weakly bounded implies strongly bounded) Let \(E\) be a Banach space. The subset \(B \subset E\) is weakly bounded, this means t \(|f(x)| \leq M_f\) when \(x \in B\) for every fixed \(f\in E^\star\), where \(M_f\) is a constant independent of \(x\). Then \(B\) is bounded.

Proof of Lemma 4 Consider continuous linear functional \(F_x\) given by \(F_x (f)=f(x)\) for all \(f \in E^\star\) when \(x\in B\). It is obviously that \(\|F_x\|=\|x\|\). It follows from weakly boundedness of \(B\) that \[ \sup_{x\in B}\|F_x(f)\| \leq M_f <+\infty, \quad f\in E^\star. \] By uniform boundedness principle, we have \(\sup_{x\in B}\|Fx\| < +\infty\), which implies \(\|x\| < +\infty\) for all \(x \in B\). The lemma is proved. \(\Box\)

If \(A\) is surjective in the theorem 2, we have further information concerning right inverse of \(A\) which is the following corollary.

Corollary 5 Under the assumption of Theorem 2, if \(\eqref{2}\) holds for some \(C>0\), and \(A\) is surjective, there exists a linear continuous operator \(G\) from \(F\) to \(E\) such that \[ A\circ G(f)=f, \quad \forall f\in F,\\ \|G(f)\|_E \leq \frac{1}{c} \|f\|_F. \] Proof of Corollary 5 It follows from \(\eqref{2}\) that \(A^\star\) is injective. According to bounded inverse theorem, there exists a bounded linear operator \((A^\star)^{-1}\) from \(R(A^\star)\) to \(F\) which satisfies \(\|(A^\star)^{-1}\|<\frac{1}{c}\). Indeed, it follows from \[ \|(A^\star)^{-1}y\| = \|(A^\star)^{-1}A^\star x\|=\|x\|\leq \frac{1}{c}\|A^\star x\|=\frac{1}{c}\|y\|, \forall y\in R(A^\star). \] Hence, we have \(I=((A^\star)^{-1}A^\star)^\star=A((A^\star)^{-1})^\star\). Letting \(G=((A^\star)^{-1})^\star\) proves the corollary. \(\Box\)
