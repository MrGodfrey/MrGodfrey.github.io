The criterion for controllability

2018-11-16 | 数学 ， 学习笔记

In this section, we are going to give the necessary and sufficient condition for the controllability of the linear control system.

We will prove that the controllability is equivalent to the reversibility of the controllability Gramian. This equivalence is called an integral criterion for controllability. We will also give the famous Kalman rank condition.

An Integral Criterion for controllability

In the notation and assumption of [1], we consider a typical time-varying linear system \[ \dot x(t)=S(t)x(t)+B(t)u(t), \quad t \in [0,T].\tag{1}\label{1} \]

We will restrict our attention to the exact controllability of the system \(\eqref{1}\).

Let \(x_0\) and \(x_1\) be in \(\mathbb{R}^n\). In order to let the above system be exactly controllable, we need to find a control \(u\), such that the solution \(x(t)\) of equation \(\eqref{1}\) with initial data \(x(0)=x_0\) satisfies \(x(T)=x_1\). If the matrix \(G=\int_0^T S(T-s)BB^TS(T-s)^Tds\) is invertible, we can let \(u\in L^{\infty}([0,T];\mathbb{R}^m)\) be defined by \[ u(s):=B^TS(T-s)^TG^{-1}(x_1-S(T)x_0), \quad s\in[0,T]. \tag{2}\label{2} \] Infact, the solution \(x(t)\) is given by \[ x(t)=S(t)x_0 +\int_0^tS(t-s)B(s)u(s)ds,\quad t\in[0,T]. \] Then \(x(T)=x_1\) follows from substituting \(\eqref{2}\) into above formula. We call \(G\) the controllability Gramian, and it is a nonnegative symmetric matrix. In fact, one can choose every \(x\in\mathbb{R}^n\), and the nonnegativity follows from \(x^TGx\geq0\). In particular, the matrix \(G\) is invertible if and only if there exists \(c>0\) such that \(x^TGx\geq c|x|^2\), for all \(x\) in \(\mathbb{R}^n\).

The condition for the controllability of \(\eqref{1}\) is given in the following theorem [2, Theorem 1.11].

Theorem 1 (integral criterion) The system \(\eqref{1}\) is controllable if and only if its controllability Gramian is invertible.

Proof of Theorem 1 By the above discussion, we only need to proof the "only if" part.

Let us assume that \(G\) is not invertible. Then there exists \(y\neq 0\) such that \(Gy=0\). In particular, \(\int_0^T|B^TS(T-s)^Ty|^2ds=0\). Hence, we imply \(y^TS(T-s)B=0, a.e. [0,T]\). The solution \(x(t)\) of equation \(\eqref{1}\) with initial data \(x(0)=0\) satisfies \(x(T)=\int_0^TS(T-s)Buds\), which implies \(y^Tx(T)\)=0. Since \(y\neq 0\), it follows that, whatever \(u\) is, \(x(T)\neq y\). This concludes the proof. \(\Box\)

It worth to point out that the control \(u\) defined by \(\eqref{2}\) has the quite interesting property given in the following proposition[2, Proposition 1.13].

Proposition 2 (Minimal energy) Let \(u\) be given by \(\eqref{2}\). Let \(x_1\in\mathbb{R}^n\) and let \(\bar{u}\) be such that the solution of \(\eqref{1}\) with initial data \(x(0)=x_0\) satisfies \(x(T)=x_1\). Then \[ \int_0^T|u(t)|^2dt\leq \int _0^T |\bar u(t)|^2dt \] with equality if and only if \(u(t)=\bar u(t)\) for almost every \(t\in[0,T]\).

Proof of Proposition 2 It is easy to check that \(\int_0^TS(T-s)B(u-\bar{u})ds=0\) and \[ \int_{0}^T |\bar u|^2ds=\int_{0}^T |u|^2ds+\int_{0}^T |\bar u-u|^2ds+2\int_{0}^T u^T (\bar u-u)ds. \]

But \(\int_{0}^T u^T(\bar u-u)ds=(x_1-S(T)x_0)^TG^{-1}\int_0^TS(T-s)B(\bar u-u)ds=0\) since \(G\) is symmetric. This concludes proof. \(\Box\).
